Setting up the Re-sourcery




- February 2019 -


It is during the construction of the dome that issues related to the generation of waste has become obvious. A random visit to the recycling shop at the Waitakere refuse centre has provided an critical statement that could not be ignored :


The 5 metres-high stack of trashed wood at the refuse centre.


This eye-opener prompted my van to visit Rollercoaster, a design studio in Kumeu. Some of their workers mentioned the potential to avoid waste of absolutely fine pieces of wood. The fast-paced production cycles of the studio generate a constant amount of offcuts that are unsuitable for the large-scale designs made there; however, the same offcuts are potentially ideal for smaller projects.

Rollercoaster staff members have been going out of their ways to contact us when wood was available, which ended up with the van going back and forth from the Pocket Lab to their studio in Kumeu, loaded with new material:


Loading Tons of donated offcuts from Rollercoaster


Quickly the wood added up, stacked under tarps and badly protecteed from the rain. They were difficult to access, and the word-of-mouth inviting anyone to help themselves with this resource was a little too quiet. The initiative needed a little more structure, so a brainstorming began.

At this local scale, and without desicion power at the design stage, the project won't be able to revolutionise the waste system by creating a full, utopian loop where no waste is created. But the project can still limit the amount of trash with little effort.


A sneaky add-on to make a dent


We were now considering an existing platform to reach out further than our community. An online page set up by EcoMatters was going to do the hard communication work on our behalf:


Existing medium to reach out to future users


Then there was a need of a storage space to keep everything in a dry space, and easy to access. As primary material, we used a large stack of pallets gathered recently, thanks to the warehouse manager of Solar City allowing us to help ourselves at their facility. The plywood top of each off them offers an easy surface to work on:


Sourcing Raw material for structural pieces


We also had a few second-hand advertisment canvases, initially gathered for summer festivals and now getting dusty and lonely:


Sourcing raw material for the cover


After that, it took a few days to put together the physical space. We ensured the wood was going to stay nice and dry, will not warp and was going to be laying on its side, therefore easy to browse though the collection and pick up :


First Design


Making material easy to access and avoid warping


Some extra beams donated from a Gribblehirst Hub members became the supporting structure of the rainproof cover:


Preparing the supporting structure for the waterproof cover


The whole structure needed to fit in the landscape of the reserve the Pocket Lab is part of; therefore, bamboo mats - also upcycled from a film set, were used to cover the walls of the storage. The trick is now to wait from climbers to invade this vertical space and settle for good, merging the structure into the green landscape:


Merging the structure within the green background
